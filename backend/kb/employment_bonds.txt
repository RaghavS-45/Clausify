DOCUMENT TYPE: Knowledge Base Reference
TOPIC: Employment Bonds and Training Cost Recovery Clauses
APPLICABLE TO: employment_contract, offer_letter, internship_agreement

---

OVERVIEW

An employment bond is a clause that requires an employee to either work for a company for a minimum period or pay a penalty (typically framed as reimbursement of "training costs") if they leave before the bond period ends.

Employment bonds are highly controversial in India and are widely used, particularly in IT services companies. Their enforceability is disputed.

---

WHAT IS AN EMPLOYMENT BOND?

A bond typically states:
"The employee agrees to serve the company for a minimum period of [X months/years]. If the employee resigns before this period, they shall pay the company ₹[amount] as liquidated damages / training cost recovery."

Common bond periods:
- Fresh graduates at IT services: 1–2 years
- Specialized training programs: 2–3 years
- After foreign onsite deployment: 1–2 years additional
- After company-funded certification/course: 1 year per ₹1 lakh of training cost

---

LEGAL STATUS OF EMPLOYMENT BONDS IN INDIA

GENERAL PRINCIPLE:
Section 27 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872: Agreements in restraint of trade are void.
Section 74: Liquidated damages are enforceable only if they represent a genuine pre-estimate of loss — not a penalty.

COURT POSITIONS:
Superintendence Company of India v. Krishan Murgai (1980, Supreme Court): Distinguished between reasonable restraint during employment and unreasonable post-employment restriction. During-employment bonds are more likely to be upheld.

Sandhya Organic Chemicals v. United Phosphorus (1997): Courts will enforce bonds where actual training costs are documented and recoverable.

Practical reality: Courts have awarded damages in some cases where companies provided specialized, documented training worth significant cost. Courts have refused to enforce bonds where training was basic and on-the-job.

WHAT MAKES A BOND MORE ENFORCEABLE:
1. Training cost was substantial, specific, and documented (not vague "orientation")
2. The employee received unique, external, certifiable training (CPA, specialized software, foreign training)
3. The bond amount is a genuine estimate of costs incurred, not punitive
4. The bond was signed with full awareness and no coercion

WHAT MAKES A BOND LESS ENFORCEABLE:
1. "Training" was just standard onboarding / orientation
2. Bond amount is arbitrary (not linked to actual training cost)
3. Employee had no real choice (take-it-or-leave-it on day 1)
4. Bond amount is disproportionately high relative to training value
5. Signing was a condition of receiving the offer (adhesion contract)

COMPANY-SPECIFIC BOND PRACTICES

TCS (Tata Consultancy Services):
Bond period: 2 years from joining
Bond amount: ₹50,000–₹75,000
Training cited: Initial training at Tata Consultancy Learning programs
Enforceability: TCS has pursued departing employees in labor courts. Employees have successfully contested. Mixed outcomes.

Infosys:
Bond period: 1 year (reduced from earlier policies)
Bond amount: ₹1,50,000
Training cited: Infosys Mysuru campus training
Enforceability: Similar to TCS — actively pursued, contested outcomes.

Wipro:
Bond period: 1 year
Bond amount: ₹75,000
Note: Wipro's training bond has been reduced in recent years as retention strategy shifted to market-rate salaries.

CTS (Cognizant):
Bond period: 1 year
Bond amount: ₹75,000

HCL Technologies:
Bond period: 1 year
Training bond: ₹50,000–₹75,000

OBSERVATION: These bonds are specifically designed for mass-hire fresher programs where the company invests in campus-to-corporate transition training. They are less common (and harder to enforce) for lateral hires who require no onboarding training.

---

POST-DEPLOYMENT / ONSITE BONDS

Some IT services companies send freshers or mid-level employees on-site to client locations (US, UK, Europe). After return, they impose additional bonds:

Standard: 1–2 year bond after international onsite deployment
Bond amount: ₹1–5 lakh
Reasoning: Company sponsored visa, travel, accommodation, and onsite allowances
Enforceability: Higher than training bonds — specific, documented cost incurred by the company

RED FLAGS IN ONSITE BONDS:
Red flag: Bond amount exceeds actual onsite deployment cost
Red flag: Bond triggers even if employee was sent onsite involuntarily
Red flag: Bond applies even if employee is terminated after return (should only apply to voluntary resignation)

---

WHAT TO DO IF PRESENTED WITH A BOND

BEFORE SIGNING:
1. Ask for a detailed breakdown of "training costs" — what specific training, what specific cost?
2. Negotiate the bond period down (1 year is more reasonable than 2)
3. Negotiate the bond amount down (proportional to actual documented costs)
4. Add a clause: Bond does not apply if the employee is terminated without cause
5. Add a clause: Bond amount reduces pro-rata (50% if you leave at 6 months of a 1-year bond)

AFTER SIGNING (IF YOU WANT TO LEAVE):
1. Calculate the bond amount vs the salary difference at the new job
2. Many bonds are negotiated down at departure — companies prefer quick resolution to litigation
3. Some new employers absorb the bond cost as part of the joining offer — ask
4. Even if the company sends a legal notice, litigation is expensive and outcomes are uncertain; companies often settle

PRO-RATA REDUCTION (BEST PRACTICE TO REQUEST):
If bond is ₹1,00,000 for 2 years, request that it reduces by ₹4,167/month for each month served.
After 12 months: Bond amount = ₹50,000 (50%)
After 18 months: Bond amount = ₹25,000 (25%)
This is entirely negotiable and not legally required, but commonly agreed to.

---

BOND VS NON-COMPETE: KEY DIFFERENCE

Bond: Restricts you from LEAVING the company without paying a penalty. Primarily enforceable.
Non-compete: Restricts you from WORKING FOR COMPETITORS after leaving. Primarily unenforceable post-employment.

A contract can contain both. Deal with them separately.

---

RED FLAGS IN BOND CLAUSES

Red flag: "Training cost" includes salary paid during training — your salary is your compensation, not the company's cost.
Red flag: Bond triggered even during probation (before confirmation) — aggressive.
Red flag: Bond applies to voluntary AND involuntary termination — should only apply to voluntary resignation.
Red flag: Bond amount is a round number (₹1,00,000) not linked to any documented training — suggests it is punitive, not compensatory.
Red flag: Bond clause buried in a 40-page contract without specific attention drawn to it.
Red flag: No pro-rata reduction clause — bond amount doesn't decrease over time even as the risk to the company decreases.
