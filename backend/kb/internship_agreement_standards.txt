---

OVERVIEW

An internship agreement defines the terms of a short-term, project-based work arrangement between a company and an intern (typically a student or recent graduate). Unlike an employment contract, an internship is generally not governed by the same labor protections, but certain statutory rights still apply.

Internships in India can be:
- Paid (stipend-based)
- Unpaid (increasingly rare; legally questionable in some contexts)
- Academic credit-linked (governed by university rules)
- Pre-placement (PPO-linked)

---

STIPEND STANDARDS

WHAT IS A STIPEND?
A stipend is a fixed periodic payment to an intern. It is not considered a "salary" and is not subject to the same statutory deductions as employment income.

STANDARD STIPEND RANGES (2024, INDIA):

Tier 1 product companies (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta):
- Summer intern: ₹80,000–₹1,50,000/month
- Note: These often include housing, transport, and relocation allowance

Indian unicorns and high-growth startups (Zepto, Razorpay, Meesho, CRED):
- ₹30,000–₹80,000/month

Mid-tier tech companies and Series A startups:
- ₹15,000–₹40,000/month

Traditional IT services (TCS, Infosys, Wipro intern programs):
- ₹5,000–₹15,000/month
- Note: Often in structured intern programs with PPO guarantees

Consulting and Big 4 (BCG, McKinsey, Deloitte, KPMG):
- ₹50,000–₹1,00,000/month

SME / Early-stage startup:
- ₹5,000–₹20,000/month
- Red flag: ₹0 stipend with no academic credit — value exchange must exist

IIT/NIT summer interns (SDE roles): ₹20,000–₹1,00,000/month depending on company

Red flag: Stipend significantly below market rate without compensating benefits (strong mentorship, publication credit, strong PPO probability)
Red flag: "Stipend may vary based on performance" — stipend should be fixed and unconditional

TAXABILITY OF STIPEND:
Stipends are taxable as "income from other sources" under the Income Tax Act if they exceed ₹2.5 lakh/year (₹20,833/month) under old regime or ₹3 lakh under new basic exemption.
Internships at research institutions (IIT, IISc) may be exempt if purely academic.

DURATION AND WORKING HOURS

STANDARD DURATION:
- Summer internship: 2–3 months
- Semester internship: 4–6 months
- Year-long internship: Rare; typically at startups; closer to a full-time role

STANDARD WORKING HOURS:
- Full-time internship: 40–45 hours/week (same as full-time employees)
- Part-time internship: 20–25 hours/week
- Red flag: "As required by project needs" with no defined hours — enables exploitation
- Red flag: Required to work weekends, nights, or overtime without additional compensation

LEAVE:
- Standard: 1–2 days paid leave per month, or equivalent
- Red flag: No leave entitlement for internships longer than 3 months
- Medical/emergency leave: Should be available without stipend deduction

---

PRE-PLACEMENT OFFER (PPO) CLAUSES

A PPO (Pre-Placement Offer) is a full-time job offer extended to an intern at the end of their internship, contingent on satisfactory performance.

STANDARD PPO TERMS:
- PPO should be in writing, with CTC, role, and joining date specified
- PPO offer deadline: Typically before the university's placement season opens (for campus interns)
- Standard validity: 2–4 weeks to accept/decline
- Conversion rate: Top companies convert 60–90% of interns; below 40% is a warning sign about work environment or intern experience

RED FLAGS IN PPO CLAUSES:
Red flag: "PPO may be offered at the company's sole discretion" with no defined criteria
Red flag: PPO CTC is significantly lower than new graduate offers for the same role (more than 15–20% gap)
Red flag: PPO offer conditional on "no other offer acceptance" — coercive
Red flag: PPO withdrawal after acceptance — becoming more common at startups during funding crunches; ensure PPO includes a joining date within 12 months

WHAT TO DO IF PPO IS WITHDRAWN:
- Check if the PPO letter includes any legally binding language
- In India, PPO withdrawal is not specifically illegal but may constitute breach of a promise (promissory estoppel)
- Practically: difficult to enforce; focus on finding alternative offers

IP AND CONFIDENTIALITY IN INTERNSHIPS

IP ASSIGNMENT:
Standard: Work product created during the internship, using company resources, and within the scope of assigned projects belongs to the company.
Red flag: "All inventions created during the internship period" — same over-broad IP claim as employment contracts.
Red flag: Academic work for credit (thesis, course project) claimed by the company.
Important: If your internship work forms part of your thesis or publication, get written confirmation that you can publish it (usually requires removal of confidential data).

CONFIDENTIALITY:
Standard: Same as employment NDAs — keep company data, client names, financials, and trade secrets confidential.
Acceptable duration: 1–2 years post-internship
Red flag: Indefinite confidentiality from an intern who saw basic company data
Red flag: Confidentiality preventing you from listing the company on your resume or describing your work in interviews — this is common at stealth startups and is unreasonable.

INTERNSHIP VS EMPLOYMENT: KEY DIFFERENCES

|Aspect|Internship|Employment|
|---|---|---|
|Statutory leave|Not mandated|Mandated (Shops Act)|
|PF contribution|Not applicable|Applicable (12% Basic)|
|Gratuity|Not applicable|Applicable after 5 years|
|Notice period|Typically 7–14 days|30–90 days|
|ESOP eligibility|Rare; only in startups|Common in tech companies|
|Maternity/paternity|Not applicable|Applicable|
|EPF/ESI registration|Not required|Required|

RED FLAGS SPECIFIC TO INTERNSHIP AGREEMENTS

Red flag: No written agreement at all
Risk: Stipend can be withheld, hours can be extended, PPO can be denied with no legal recourse.

Red flag: Restrictive non-compete after internship
Legal status: Same as employment — post-internship non-compete is largely unenforceable under Section 27.
Common at: Consulting firms, agencies, boutique firms that fear interns will start competing ventures.

Red flag: Company claims ownership of academic work or research done at university
This is clearly over-broad and likely unenforceable.

Red flag: Required to sign a bond (indemnity/training cost recovery)
Some companies ask interns to sign that they will pay back "training costs" if they don't join full-time.
Standard: Unenforceable and exploitative. Training cost recovery clauses generally cannot force you to pay back "soft costs" like mentor time.

Red flag: No defined manager or mentor
An internship with no defined reporting structure is often a sign of poor program quality and an indication that the PPO rate is low.

---

CHECKLIST BEFORE SIGNING AN INTERNSHIP AGREEMENT

- [ ] Is the stipend amount, payment date, and payment method clearly stated?
- [ ] Is the duration clearly defined (start date, end date)?
- [ ] Are working hours and leave entitlement defined?
- [ ] Is the role/project description specific enough to be meaningful on your resume?
- [ ] Is the IP assignment limited to work done during the internship using company resources?
- [ ] Is there a confidentiality duration defined (not indefinite)?
- [ ] If PPO is promised/expected, is there a written commitment?
- [ ] Is there a non-compete clause? If so, challenge it — it is very likely unenforceable.
- [ ] Can you publish/present your work for academic purposes?
- [ ] Is there a defined process for stipend disputes or termination?
