OVERVIEW

ESOPs (Employee Stock Ownership Plans) and stock options are a way for companies to give employees partial ownership of the company as part of their compensation. In startups, equity can be a significant part of total compensation. In listed companies, stock grants may vest quarterly or annually.

Understanding ESOP terms is critical because vesting schedules, cliff periods, and exercise windows directly affect the value of your equity.

---

KEY ESOP TERMINOLOGY

OPTION: The right (not obligation) to buy company shares at a pre-agreed price (exercise/strike price) at a future date.

GRANT: The initial allocation of options (e.g., "1,000 options granted at ₹10/share strike price").

STRIKE PRICE (EXERCISE PRICE): The price at which you can buy the shares. Set at the time of the grant. Lower than current market value = more valuable grant.

VESTING: The process of earning your options over time. You cannot exercise unvested options.

CLIFF: A minimum duration before any options vest. Standard: 1-year cliff.

VESTING SCHEDULE: The timeline over which options vest. Standard: 4-year vest with 1-year cliff (25% vests after year 1, then ~2.08% per month for the next 3 years).

EXERCISE WINDOW: The time period after leaving a company during which you can exercise vested options. Standard: 90 days post-departure. After this window, unvested options expire.

LIQUIDATION EVENT: When equity turns into money — typically an IPO or acquisition.

FAIR MARKET VALUE (FMV): For unlisted companies, FMV is determined by a registered valuer. You pay tax on the difference between FMV and strike price when you exercise options.

---

STANDARD ESOP TERMS (STARTUP CONTEXT, INDIA)

GRANT SIZE:
- Fresher/Entry SDE: 0.01%–0.05% of fully diluted shares
- Mid-level SDE: 0.05%–0.2%
- Senior SDE/Tech Lead: 0.1%–0.5%
- Director/VP Engineering: 0.3%–1%
- CTO/C-suite: 0.5%–3%

Red flag: "Options worth ₹X at current valuation" — this inflates perceived value; what matters is percentage of fully diluted shares, not rupee value at a moment-in-time valuation.

VESTING SCHEDULE:
Standard: 4-year total vest, 1-year cliff
- After 12 months: 25% (cliff vest)
- Months 13–48: 2.08% per month
Aggressive (pro-company): 5–6 year total vest
Employee-friendly: 4-year vest with no cliff (monthly from day 1)
Red flag: No vesting schedule described — options may never vest
Red flag: Vesting contingent on "satisfactory performance" with no defined criteria

EXERCISE WINDOW:
Standard: 90 days from last working day to exercise vested options
Best practice: 5–10 years (becoming more common at employee-friendly startups)
Red flag: 30 days exercise window — nearly impossible to arrange funding and complete transaction
Red flag: Options automatically cancelled on resignation, regardless of vesting — this means you earn options but never get to use them

ANTI-DILUTION PROTECTION:
Standard: Not provided to employees (only investors get this)
Best practice: Grant letter mentions how dilution from future funding rounds will affect your percentage
Red flag: No mention of dilution — your percentage will shrink with every funding round with no protection

TAXATION OF ESOPs (INDIA)

Two taxable events:

1. EXERCISE (When you buy shares at strike price):
Tax triggered: Difference between FMV and strike price is taxed as "perquisite" = treated as salary income = taxed at your income tax slab rate
Example: Strike price ₹10, FMV ₹100, you exercise 1,000 options → Perquisite = (100-10) × 1000 = ₹90,000 → Taxed at slab (up to 30%)

Special rule for unlisted startups (DPIIT registered):
Tax on exercise can be deferred for 5 years or until sale, whichever is earlier. This is a significant benefit — confirm eligibility.

2. SALE (When you sell the shares):
Short-term capital gains (STCG): If held < 24 months — taxed at slab rate
Long-term capital gains (LTCG): If held ≥ 24 months — taxed at 20% with indexation (unlisted) or 12.5% above ₹1.25 lakh (listed, new regime FY25)

Practical implication: Tax on exercise can be a significant immediate cash burden. If FMV is high and you exercise a large number of options, you owe tax before you've sold the shares. This is called the "AMT trap" in the US; India's deferral for startups partially addresses this.

---

WHAT TO LOOK FOR IN AN ESOP GRANT LETTER

A proper ESOP grant letter must include:
1. Number of options granted
2. Class of shares (ordinary, preference — preference shares usually held by investors; employees get ordinary/equity)
3. Strike price
4. Grant date
5. Vesting schedule (start date, cliff, monthly rate)
6. Exercise window post-departure
7. Conditions for acceleration (e.g., full acceleration on acquisition)
8. What happens to unvested options on termination (resignation vs firing vs acquisition)
9. Governing plan document reference (the ESOP pool terms)

Red flag: Offer letter mentions ESOPs but no formal grant letter is issued — you have no legal entitlement.
Red flag: Grant letter says "options subject to board approval" — options are not official until board-approved.

---

ACCELERATION CLAUSES

Single-trigger acceleration: All unvested options vest immediately upon a change of control (acquisition/IPO).
Double-trigger acceleration: Options accelerate only if there is (a) a change of control AND (b) you are terminated without cause.

Standard: Double-trigger is the norm for most employees. Single-trigger is rare and very favorable.
Red flag: No acceleration clause — acquirer can cancel all unvested options with no compensation.

---

ESOP RED FLAGS SUMMARY

- No formal grant letter (only mentioned in offer letter)
- Options described in rupee value, not percentage of fully diluted shares
- Vesting schedule not specified
- Exercise window of 30 days post-departure
- Options cancelled on resignation regardless of vesting
- No mention of what happens to ESOPs in an acquisition
- No acceleration clause
- Vesting contingent on discretionary performance assessment
- Strike price at or above FMV (no intrinsic value)
