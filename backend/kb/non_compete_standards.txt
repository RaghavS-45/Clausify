OVERVIEW

Non-compete clauses restrict an employee from working for a competitor after leaving the company. Non-solicitation clauses restrict an employee from poaching clients or colleagues after leaving.

In India, post-employment non-compete clauses are largely unenforceable. Section 27 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 states that "every agreement by which anyone is restrained from exercising a lawful profession, trade, or business of any kind is to that extent void."

However, this does not mean you should ignore these clauses. Companies use them to:
(a) Intimidate employees into not joining competitors
(b) Sue for injunctions even if enforcement is unlikely
(c) Blacklist employees in smaller, interconnected industries

---

LEGAL STATUS OF NON-COMPETE IN INDIA

POST-EMPLOYMENT NON-COMPETE:
Status: Generally void and unenforceable under Section 27, Indian Contract Act
Key precedents:
- Niranjan Shankar Golikari v. Century Spinning (1967): Supreme Court upheld non-compete only for the duration of employment, not post-employment
- Percept D'Mark v. Zaheer Khan (2006): Bombay HC refused to enforce post-employment non-compete
- Desiccant Rotors International v. Bappaditya Sarkar (2009): Delhi HC limited non-compete enforcement to employment period

DURING-EMPLOYMENT NON-COMPETE:
Status: Fully enforceable — you cannot work for a competitor while employed
Scope: Applies to direct competitors, moonlighting, freelancing for competing clients
Red flag: "Competitor" defined so broadly it covers any technology company

NON-SOLICITATION (POST-EMPLOYMENT):
Status: More likely to be enforced than non-compete, especially for client relationships
Courts have upheld non-solicitation of specific named clients for limited periods
Standard: 6–12 months, limited to clients you actually worked with

NON-DISCLOSURE (PERMANENT):
Status: Fully enforceable, even post-employment
Covers: Trade secrets, customer lists, pricing data, proprietary technology
Standard: Should be limited to information that is actually confidential, not general industry knowledge

---

STANDARD NON-COMPETE CLAUSE PARAMETERS

DURATION:
- Standard (if included): 6 months post-employment
- Acceptable: Up to 12 months for senior roles with access to critical trade secrets
- Red flag: More than 12 months
- Red flag: Indefinite or "as long as the information remains confidential"

GEOGRAPHIC SCOPE:
- Standard: Limited to specific cities or regions where the company operates
- Acceptable: India-wide for companies with national operations
- Red flag: Worldwide — unenforceable and excessive
- Red flag: No geographic limitation specified

INDUSTRY SCOPE:
- Standard: Limited to direct competitors in the same product/service category
- Acceptable: Named list of specific competitor companies
- Red flag: "Any company in the technology sector" — too broad to be enforceable
- Red flag: "Any business that competes with any current or future product of the company"

COMPENSATION:
- Best practice: Company should pay a "garden salary" or compensation during the non-compete period
- Standard in India: No compensation paid during non-compete (most clauses are unenforceable anyway)
- If no compensation is paid, enforceability drops further in courts

---

STANDARD NON-SOLICITATION CLAUSE PARAMETERS

CLIENT NON-SOLICITATION:
- Standard duration: 6–12 months
- Standard scope: Clients you personally worked with, not all company clients
- Red flag: "All present or future clients of the company" — over-broad
- Red flag: Duration exceeding 24 months

EMPLOYEE NON-SOLICITATION:
- Standard duration: 6–12 months
- Standard scope: Not approaching employees you directly managed or worked with
- Red flag: Blanket restriction on approaching any company employee — over-broad
- Red flag: Duration exceeding 12 months

---

RED FLAGS IN NON-COMPETE LANGUAGE

HIGH RISK LANGUAGE:
"The employee shall not, directly or indirectly, engage in any business activity that competes with or is similar to the company's business for a period of [X years] anywhere in the world."
→ Unenforceable but aggressive. Signals company has a history of threatening departing employees.

"Competing business" means any entity that offers products or services that are the same as or similar to the company's."
→ "Similar to" is dangerously broad. Similar to WhatsApp? Any messaging app. Similar to Excel? Any spreadsheet tool.

"The employee shall not engage any current or prospective client of the company."
→ "Prospective" clients = anyone the company has ever contacted. Effectively bars you from the industry.

"This restriction applies even if the employee is terminated without cause."
→ You can be fired, receive no compensation, and still be bound by a non-compete. Extremely unfair.

ACCEPTABLE LANGUAGE (BENCHMARK):
"For a period of 6 months following the end of employment, the employee shall not directly solicit or approach [specific named competitors] for employment in a role substantially similar to their role at the company, within the territory of [specific states/cities]."
→ Time-bound, geographically limited, scope-limited, and employer-specific.

---

WHAT TO DO IF YOUR CONTRACT HAS A BROAD NON-COMPETE

1. Ask for the clause to be removed — post-employment non-competes are unenforceable in India, and many startups will simply delete the clause on request.
2. Ask for it to be narrowed — specific geography, specific competitors, shorter duration.
3. Consult a lawyer before joining if the clause is unusually aggressive.
4. Know that Indian courts rarely grant injunctions against departing employees and almost never grant damages unless specific trade secret theft is provable.
5. Document that you are not taking any proprietary documents, code, or data when you leave.

MOONLIGHTING CLAUSES

Many contracts include an exclusivity or moonlighting clause that restricts outside work during employment.

Standard: Restriction on working for direct competitors during employment — enforceable.
Red flag: "Employee shall not engage in any work, paid or unpaid, outside company employment" — too broad.
Red flag: Restriction extends post-employment — unenforceable.

Companies like Wipro, Infosys, and IBM have terminated employees for moonlighting. Companies like Swiggy, Paytm, and several startups have explicitly allowed moonlighting with disclosure.

Best practice: Disclose any outside work to your manager or HR in writing. Keep it outside company hours. Never use company resources.
