WHAT IS AN NDA?

A Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) is a legally binding contract where one or more parties agree to keep certain information confidential. NDAs are common in business relationships, employment, vendor contracts, and pre-investment discussions.

NDAs are fully enforceable in India under the Indian Contract Act, 1872 (Sections 23 and 27), provided the clause is reasonable in scope and duration.

---

TYPES OF NDAs

1. MUTUAL NDA (MNDA / BILATERAL NDA):
Both parties agree to keep each other's information confidential.
Used for: Business discussions, potential partnerships, M&A due diligence, investor conversations.
Standard: Both parties are equally bound. Neither can disclose the other's confidential information.
Risk level for individual: LOW — you are both disclosing and receiving information.

2. ONE-WAY NDA (UNILATERAL NDA):
Only one party (the recipient) agrees to keep the disclosing party's information confidential.
Used for: Vendors receiving client data, employees receiving company IP, freelancers receiving project details.
Standard: The disclosing party has no obligation to keep your information confidential.
Risk level for individual: MEDIUM — you are bound; the other party is not.
Red flag: A one-way NDA when both parties are sharing sensitive information — you should have a mutual NDA in this case.

3. EMPLOYMENT NDA (within employment contract):
NDA embedded in an employment agreement governing confidentiality during and after employment.
Covered separately in the IP and Confidentiality document.

---

STANDARD NDA TERMS

DEFINITION OF CONFIDENTIAL INFORMATION:
Standard: Specifically defined categories — technical data, business plans, customer lists, pricing, financial projections, source code, proprietary processes.
Acceptable: "Information marked as confidential at the time of disclosure."
Red flag: "All information disclosed in any form, whether or not marked confidential."
→ Makes every casual conversation potentially a breach.

Red flag: "All information that a reasonable person would consider confidential."
→ Vague and subjective; creates uncertainty about what is covered.

EXCLUSIONS FROM CONFIDENTIALITY (MUST BE PRESENT):
A well-drafted NDA always excludes:
1. Information already in the public domain (not through breach)
2. Information the recipient knew before disclosure (provable by prior records)
3. Information independently developed by the recipient without reference to disclosed information
4. Information received from a third party without confidentiality restrictions
5. Information required to be disclosed by law, court order, or regulation

Red flag: NDA with no exclusions — everything becomes "confidential" with no escape.

DURATION:
Standard: 2–5 years for business NDAs
Standard for trade secrets: Indefinite (trade secret protection lasts as long as the information remains secret)
Acceptable for sensitive IP: 5–7 years
Red flag: Indefinite duration for general confidential information (not trade secrets)
Red flag: Duration starting from the date of the agreement rather than the date of disclosure — can be triggered retroactively

PERMITTED DISCLOSURES:
Standard: Confidential information can be shared with employees, advisors, and contractors who need to know it, provided they are bound by equivalent confidentiality obligations.
Red flag: "No disclosure to any third party under any circumstances" — prevents you from getting legal advice about the NDA itself.

RETURN OR DESTRUCTION OF INFORMATION:
Standard: On termination of the relationship, confidential information must be returned or destroyed within 30 days, with written certification.
Red flag: No return/destruction clause — information can accumulate with the recipient indefinitely.

---

RED FLAGS IN NDAs

SCOPE RED FLAGS:

Red flag: NDA covering information that the recipient already knew or publicly knew
Problem: Unenforceable in that scope, but creates confusion and chilling effect.

Red flag: NDA claiming to cover "any information related to the company's past, present, or future business"
Problem: Indefinitely and impossibly broad.

Red flag: "Confidential information includes the existence of this NDA itself."
Acceptable in limited cases (M&A discussions, sensitive negotiations).
Red flag if standard: You may not be able to tell a future employer that you signed an NDA with a former client — this can block future work.

REMEDY RED FLAGS:

Red flag: "Breach of this NDA entitles the disclosing party to injunctive relief and liquidated damages of ₹[large amount] per breach."
Liquidated damages clauses in NDAs are enforceable in India if the amount is a genuine pre-estimate of loss. If the amount is punitive, courts may reduce it.
Watch for: ₹50 lakh or more in liquidated damages for a small vendor NDA — disproportionate and designed to intimidate.

Red flag: "Recipient waives the right to challenge the validity of this NDA."
Not fully enforceable but creates friction.

ASSIGNMENT RED FLAGS:

Red flag: "This NDA may be assigned by the disclosing party to any successor entity."
Problem: If the company is acquired, your NDA obligations automatically transfer to the acquirer, potentially binding you to a competitor.
Standard: Assignment should require your consent, or at least notification.

---

JURISDICTION AND GOVERNING LAW

Standard: NDA should specify which country/state's laws govern and where disputes will be resolved.
Red flag: Governing law is a foreign jurisdiction (e.g., Delaware, UK) for a purely domestic Indian business arrangement — unnecessary and creates enforcement complications.
Standard for India: Specify "Courts of [City], [State], India" and "Laws of India."
Red flag: Mandatory arbitration in a foreign seat (e.g., Singapore, London) for a small business NDA — expensive and impractical for the individual.

---

WHAT TO NEGOTIATE IN AN NDA

1. Change one-way to mutual if you are also sharing sensitive information
2. Narrow the definition of "confidential information" to specific categories
3. Ensure all standard exclusions are present
4. Shorten duration to 2–3 years for non-trade-secret information
5. Add a specific purpose clause: "Information may only be used for [specific purpose], not for any other purpose"
6. Remove or cap liquidated damages
7. Ensure governing law and jurisdiction are within India
8. Add a "residual knowledge" clause: "Information retained in unaided memory of individuals who had legitimate access is not subject to this NDA" — protects employees who subconsciously retain information

NDA IN HIRING / RECRUITMENT CONTEXT

Pre-employment NDAs (before receiving an offer):
Used for: Sharing technical assessments, proprietary test cases, unreleased product details.
Standard: 12–24 months, limited to information disclosed in the hiring process.
Red flag: Pre-employment NDA that binds you to confidentiality about the company's products even if you don't join.

Interview assignment NDA:
If asked to do a take-home project, ensure:
- You retain the IP of the work you create (it is not employment; the company should not own your solution)
- The NDA covers only the problem statement, not your solution approach

Red flag: "Any work product submitted during the interview process becomes the property of the company."
Problem: You could create valuable IP during an unpaid interview task that the company uses without paying you.
